## LINKEDIN

### LUNDI 16/03 — 17h30 — ✅ PUBLIÉ

I audited 40 SaaS landing pages over the past few weeks.

Not quick glances — structured audits across 8 conversion categories.

Here's what the data revealed:

Average score: 72.7 out of 100.

Sounds acceptable. It's not.

The weakest category across all 40 pages? Social proof. Average: 60.1/100.

That means most SaaS founders are asking visitors to trust them — without giving them a single reason to.

The three most common conversion leaks:

→ Form friction (43% of pages) — too many fields, too much commitment upfront
→ Weak social proof (40%) — buried below the fold or missing entirely
→ Slow performance (33%) — every extra second costs you visitors who will never come back

The gap between the lowest score (25/100) and the highest (82/100) tells the real story. The difference isn't design talent or budget. It's awareness. The best pages aren't prettier — they just know where they leak.

Most founders I talked to expected their page to score 85+.

The data disagreed.

SaaS founders — when was the last time you tested your landing page against actual conversion criteria, not just aesthetics?

### MARDI 17/03 — 17h30 — ✅ SCHEDULÉ

57% of the pages I audited have a CTA problem. And almost nobody is talking about it.

I analyzed 40 SaaS landing pages across 8 conversion categories.

The average CTA score: 65.1 out of 100.

That's lower than headline clarity. Lower than page structure. And it affects more pages than any other category — 57%.

For context, social proof averaged 60.1. But weak CTAs touch a wider surface.

Here's the pattern I keep seeing:

Founders spend weeks on the headline. They obsess over the hero image. They tweak the color palette three times.

Then they drop "Get Started" on the button and move on.

Two pages out of 40 had no primary CTA above the fold at all. The visitor had to scroll to find out what to do next.

"Get Started" tells the visitor nothing. Started with what? "Sign Up" commits them before they understand the value. "Learn More" delays the decision without a reason.

The best CTAs I saw in these audits did one thing differently: they finished the visitor's sentence. Not "Sign Up" — but what happens after they sign up.

The CTA isn't a button. It's the last chance to answer "why should I act now?"

SaaS founders — what does your main CTA actually promise to the person clicking it?

### JEUDI 19/03 — 17h30 — ✅ SCHEDULÉ

The most qualified traffic on your site lands on the worst page.

I audited 40 SaaS landing pages across 8 conversion categories.

The average score: 72.7/100.

The average pricing page score: 57.3/100.

That's a 15-point gap — on the one page where visitors have already decided they're interested and just need a reason to commit.

The lowest score in all 40 audits? A pricing page. 25/100.

The highest? Also a pricing page. 82/100.

Same page type. Same job to do. Wildly different execution.

Here's why this keeps happening:

Founders build the homepage first. They iterate on it. They test the headline, the hero, the social proof. By the time they get to pricing, they're tired. They drop a three-column table, add a "Start Free Trial" button, and ship.

But the visitor on your pricing page isn't browsing. They're comparing. They're doing math. They're looking for a reason to say yes — or an excuse to leave.

Weak CTAs, confusing plan hierarchy, and form friction on that page don't just lose a visitor.

They lose the visitor who was closest to paying.

The homepage gets your attention. The pricing page gets your revenue.

How much time did you actually spend on yours?
