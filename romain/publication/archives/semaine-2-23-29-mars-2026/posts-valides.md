## TWITTER

### LUNDI 23/03 — 15h — ⏳ À SCHEDULER

I've now audited 55+ landing pages. The one leak that shows up every single time: social proof.

15 pages last week. 15 out of 15 scored social proof as their weakest category. Not most. All of them. Scores from 10/100 to 70/100.

The pattern is always the same. The testimonials exist. The logos exist. They're just below the fold where nobody scrolls.

The fix isn't "get more reviews." It's move the proof you already have to where the visitor sees it before they start doubting.

Where do you put your social proof — above or below the fold?

### MARDI 24/03 — 15h — ⏳ À SCHEDULER

Week 1 I posted 41 replies in 3 days. Cold audits, engagement comments, value-driven stuff. Every one personalized, data-backed, zero copypaste.

Result: most of those replies got 0-3 impressions. Invisible in the threads. The people I was replying to probably never saw them.

Meanwhile my standalone posts hit 15-35 impressions each. Same account, same day, same content quality. The algorithm wasn't penalizing my content. It was penalizing the behavior — new account, 12 followers, high reply volume. It looked like a bot. It wasn't, but the system doesn't ask.

The expensive lesson: on a new account, distribution isn't a given. It's a variable. And for replies, that variable was zero.

Week 2 shift: fewer replies, more original posts. Rebuild the trust score before scaling back up.

Best content strategy in the world means nothing if the platform won't show it. Anyone else hit this wall early on?

### MERCREDI 25/03 — 15h — ⏳ À SCHEDULER

I've audited 55+ landing pages in the last two weeks. The pattern that surprised me most has nothing to do with conversion.

It's how founders react to a number vs an opinion.

Tell someone "you should add testimonials" and they say thanks and do nothing. Show them "Social Proof: 30/100" and they fix it that day.

The pages with the worst scores generated the best conversations. A 45/100 turned into a two-day back and forth about what to fix first. A 77/100 got a "thanks" and silence.

The CRO industry runs on subjective opinions. "I think your CTA should be bigger." "Maybe try a different headline." Founders hear that and file it under "later."

A score isn't perfect. But it turns "I should probably improve my page" into "I went from 62 to 78 after moving social proof above the fold." One is a feeling. The other is progress.

Do you measure your landing page or just guess?

### JEUDI 26/03 — 15h — ⏳ À SCHEDULER

I looked at the pricing pages in my dataset. One pattern keeps showing up: founders use the same CTA on every tier.

"Get Started." "Get Started." "Get Started."

Three plans, three identical buttons, zero guidance. The visitor already decided to buy — they came to the pricing page. Now they're stuck figuring out WHICH plan, and every option looks the same.

The pages that scored highest on pricing had one thing in common: each tier had a CTA that reflected its value, not just the action. "Try it free" on the starter. "Scale your workflow" on the pro. "Talk to us" on enterprise. The button tells the visitor where they belong.

57.3/100 average on pricing pages across 55+ audits. The homepage average is 72.7. Same companies. The page where the visitor is most ready to pay gets the least conversion effort.

When was the last time you read your own pricing page CTAs out loud?

### JEUDI 26/03 — 17h30 — ⏳ À SCHEDULER

Your best traffic lands on a page
with no trust signals, no differentiation,
and three identical buttons saying "Get Started."

I've audited 40+ SaaS landing pages
across 8 conversion categories.
The pricing pages averaged 57.3/100.
The homepages averaged 72.7.
That's a 15-point gap on the same sites,
the same products, the same companies.

I covered that gap last week.
This time I want to talk about what's actually broken
inside those pricing pages,
because the patterns are remarkably consistent.

The first one: trust signals vanish.
The homepage has client logos, security badges,
satisfaction guarantees.
The visitor clicks through to pricing
and all of it disappears.
The page that needs the most trust has the least.
The visitor was reassured 10 seconds ago.
Now they're staring at a price
with nothing backing it up.

The second: tiers that look identical.
Three columns, similar feature lists, different price tags.
No recommended plan.
No clear reason to pick one over another.
The visitor isn't comparing your product
to a competitor anymore.
They're stuck comparing your plans to each other,
and you're not helping them decide.

The third: every button says the same thing.
"Get Started" on the free tier.
"Get Started" on the pro tier.
"Get Started" on the enterprise tier.
The CTA describes a generic action
instead of reflecting the value
of what the visitor is buying.
"Start Growing" and "Get Started" ask for the same click
but carry completely different weight.

The fourth: social proof that was present on the homepage
is completely absent on the pricing page.
The visitor who was reading testimonials two scrolls ago
now sees nothing but a table and a button.
The proof disappears at the exact moment
the visitor is doing the math
on whether to trust you with their money.

The most extreme case I audited:
homepage 82/100, pricing page 25/100.
Same site.
The homepage was polished, clear, convincing.
The pricing page looked like it was shipped
on a Friday afternoon and never touched again.

That's probably exactly what happened.

Nobody measures pricing pages separately.
They get bundled into "the site"
and the homepage gets all the iteration.
But the visitor on your pricing page isn't browsing.
They've already decided they're interested.
They're looking for a reason to commit
or an excuse to leave.
Every missing element is an excuse.

The fix isn't a redesign.
It's making sure the pricing page has the same trust signals
as the homepage,
that each tier has a clear reason to exist,
that the CTA tells the visitor what they get
instead of what they do,
and that the proof follows the visitor
all the way to the decision point
instead of stopping at the hero section.

Here's a quick check:
open your pricing page right now
and compare it to your homepage.
Are the trust signals still there?
Is the social proof still visible?
Or does everything disappear
the moment your visitor is ready to pay?
